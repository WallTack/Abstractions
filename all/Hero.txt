								<ul class="meanings">
									<li><span class="tSpeech">n</span> <span class="term">hero</span> <span class="exp">In classical mythology, a superior being, distinguished from ordinary men chiefly by greater physical strength, courage, and ability, at the time of the Homeric poems still regarded as mortal, but from the time of Hesiod (about the eighth century b. c.) regarded as intermediate in nature between gods and men (a demigod), and immortal. Except in the case of Hercules, the Greek cult of heroes was essentially local, each country, region, or even town holding its own in especial honor. Thus Theseus was the national hero of Attica, Ajax was especially honored in Salamis, Amphiaraus at Orchomenus; while Lycurgus became a hero in Sparta, and Hesiod himself in Bœotia. The ancient veneration of heroes was to some extent parallel with that now paid to the saints of Christianity.</span> </li><li><span class="tSpeech">n</span> <span class="term">hero</span> <span class="exp">A man of distinguished valor, intrepidity, or enterprise in danger; a prominent or central personage in any remarkable action or event; one who exhibits extraordinary courage, firmness, fortitude, or intellectual greatness in any course of action.</span> </li><li><span class="tSpeech">n</span> <span class="term">hero</span> <span class="exp">The principal male personage in a poem, play, or story, or the person who has the chief place and share in the transactions related, as Achilles in the Iliad, Odysseus (Ulysses) in the Odyssey, Æneas in the Æneid.</span> </li><li><span class="tSpeech">n</span> <span class="term">hero</span> <span class="exp">A person regarded as heroic; one invested by opinion with heroic qualities.</span> </li>
