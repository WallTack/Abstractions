				<div class="usageContainer">
										<div class="usage">
						Now suppose there is also a perceiver that has no direct perception of the time t but can only perceive the state i without knowing what t is, getting the corresponding perception pi . 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						For simplicity, assume that the perception of each distinct state is distinct, so pi = pj if and only if i = j . 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						But because the perceiver has no direct perception of t, for the perceiver only the perceptions pi and their time fractions fi are relevant. 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						Although all of this is completely deterministic, for the perceiver it can be interpreted as if it has the perception pi with probability fi . 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						One might be interested not merely in the probabilities of the individual perceptions pi , but also in various sets of them, say S . 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						One can combine sets of perceptions by the AND (∧) and OR (∨) operations. 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						Although i(t), and hence also R(t), which just encodes the information in i(t) in a different form, is not directly relevant to the perceiver, which has no access to the time t, the time-average of R(t), namely ρ, is relevant to the perceiver by giving the measure of the various perceptions that the perceiver has. 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						Thus we have expressed the hypothetical probabilities (actually fractions of an unperceived time), for a set S of perceptions in this classical model, by Eq. (10), the trace of the product of a diagonal pro jection matrix and a diagonal density matrix. 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						It is only when one hypothesizes a random sample of a set S of perceptions, here represented by the pro jection operator P (S ), that one gets by Eq. (10) the probability f (S ) = tr(P(S)ρ), the measure of the set S represented by P (S ). 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						If the quantum system and the perceptions of it are restricted by superselection rules for some conserved quantity, then in a basis of eigenstates of this quantity, the relevant density matrix ρ is restricted to have all off-diagonal terms zero that connect different values of the conserved quantity. 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						Then f (S ) = tr(P(S)ρ) given by Eq. (10) would no longer directly be a probability but could be reinterpreted to be the measure for the set S of perceptions. 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						Thus in this case one can say that a density matrix ρ for the quantum system induces, by the normalized form of Eq. (10), a probability distribution over the space of sets of perceptions. 
					</div>
					<div class="uAuthor">
						Quantum Mechanics as a Simple Generalization of Classical Mechanics
					</div>
										<div class="usage">
						If f (M ) is not ﬁnite, one cannot deﬁne a normalized probability distribution over the full set M of perceptions, but one can still do it over some subset, say M ′ , if f (M ′ ) is ﬁnite. 
					</div>
